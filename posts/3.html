Operator theory - Spectral Theory Introduction

Preliminary Results
Let's start with some definitions. Let $A$ be an operator from a vector space $X$ into a vector space $Y$. We define the kernel of A to be: $$ \text{Ker}(A) := \{x \in X: Ax = 0 \} $$ and the range of A: $$ \text{Ran}(A) := \{Ax : x \in X\} $$ It is clear that the kernel is a linear subspace of X, while the range is a linear subspace of Y. We are ready to introduce an important theorem.
Theorem: Let X and Y be Banach spaces and $B \in \mathcal{B}(X, Y)$ with $\text{Ker}(B) = {0}$ and $\text{Ran(B)} = Y$, then the inverse operator $B^{-1} : Y \mapsto X \in \mathcal{B}(Y, X)$ (is bounded). What this theorem says is that a bijective bounded linear operator has a bounded inverse. Before defining the spectrum, we also have to prove some results for inverse bounded operators.
By $I$ we denote the Identity operator ($Ix = x \forall x \in X$).
Let X be a Banach space, $B \in \mathcal{B}(X)$ and $||B|| < 1$, then $I-B$ is invertible, $$(I-B)^{-1} = \sum \limits_{n=0}^{\infty} B^n$$ and $||(I-B)^{-1}|| \leq \frac{1}{1 - ||B||}$.
Proof: It follows that from the fact that for two operators A and B then $||AB|| \leq ||A||||B||$ that $$||B^n|| \leq ||B^{n-1}||||B||| \leq ... \leq ||B||^n$$ From the fact that $||B|| < 1$, we get that the series $\sum \limits_{n=0}^{\infty} ||B||^n$ converges absolutely, and since X is a Banach space, any absolutely convergent series converges so we get that $\sum \limits_{n=0}^{\infty} B^n$ is also convergent. This is because: $$\sum \limits_{n=0}^{\infty} ||B^n|| \leq \sum \limits_{n=0}^{\infty} ||B||^n$$ from the inequality shown above. Now let's prove that the inverse exists, to show that $S = (I - B)^{-1}$ we must show that $S(I-B) = (I-B)^{-1}(I-B) = I$: $$ S(I - B) = \sum \limits_{n=0}^{\infty} B^n (I - B) = \sum \limits_{n=0}^{\infty} B^n - \sum \limits_{n=0}^{\infty} B^{n+1} = I $$ (similar argument works for $(I-B)S$) To summarise, we just showed that $(I-B)^{-1}(I-B) = I$ and thus that the series is indeed equal to that inverse operator. The inequality can be easily proven with the well known geometric series result. QED.
The spectrum
Let X be a Banach space and $B \in \mathcal{B}(X)$. The resolvent set $\rho(B)$ of the operator B is the set of all $\lambda \in \mathbb{C}$ such that $B - \lambda I$ is invertible. The spectrum $\sigma(B)$ is the complement of this set: $\sigma(B) = \mathbb{C} \setminus \rho(B)$. We can therefore see that all eigenvalues of B belong to $\sigma(B)$.
Proof: Suppose $\lambda$ is an eigenvalue for a respective eigenvector $\psi$ ($\psi \neq 0$). Then: $(B - \lambda I)\psi = 0$, but since we also have that $(B - \lambda I)0 = 0$ we notice that the operator $(B - \lambda I)\psi$ is not injective and thus not invertible and thus part of $\sigma(B)$. QED. Note that the set of eigenvalues may be smaller than the spectrum of B.
The spectral radius $r(B)$ is defined to be: $$r(B) := \sup\{|\lambda|: \lambda \in \sigma(B)\}$$ Therefore the spectral radius is the minimal ball centered at 0 containing the spectrum. We also get that (the proof is quite involved). $$ r(B) = \lim_{n \to \infty} ||B^n||^{\frac{1}{n}} $$ For completeness, I will mention adjoint operators, which for an operator $A \in \mathcal{B}(X, Y)$ is defined to be $A^* : Y^* \mapsto X^*$ by: $$(A^*g)(x) := g(Ax), \forall g \in Y^*, \forall x \in X$$ and a projection which is an idempotent operator ($A = A^2$). To conclude, let's introduce compact linear operators. A linear operator $T: X \mapsto Y$ is called compact if it maps closed subsets of X to relatively compact sets of Y (relatively compact means that the closure of the set is compact).
